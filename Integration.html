Understanding the Integration of Self

The integration of my different parts, Philosophical, Socio-anthropological, and Psychological, shows a complete picture of who I am. Instead of thinking about these parts separately, I see them as connected pieces that shapes my identity, values, and behavior.

My philosophical part gives me the base of my core beliefs and values through Socrates' idea of "Know Thyself." This self-awareness helps me keep thinking about my purpose and meaning. At the same time, my socio-anthropological part, based on Cooley's "Looking Glass Self," reminds me that I exist in relation to other people and that society helps shape who I am. Lastly, my psychological part, based on Erikson's psychosocial theory, accepts that my personality and skills have grown through different life stages and challenges.

How These Selves Work Together

Integrating these three parts of me makes a good balance in my life. When I have to make choices or deal with problems, I use all three. My philosophical side helps me find what feels real and true to what I believe in. My socio-anthropological side helps me see how my choices affect my relationships and my place in society. My psychological side makes me aware of my feelings, what I've been through, and how I've grown.

For example, when I decided to step away from trying to fit into the "cool" group in elementary school, it wasn't just a social decision. It was a philosophical choice to honor who I truly am (Know Thyself), a recognition that I don't need others' validation to define myself (Looking Glass Self), and an understanding of my psychological need for originality and self-acceptance. This integration shows that personal growth isn't about distinguishing different aspects of myself, but rather about understanding how they complement and strengthen each other.
